🔤 Jargon Cheat Sheet

50 terms you'll hear constantly at Paramount. Learn 5 a day, impress everyone.

LLM
Large Language Model, the brain behind ChatGPT, Claude, etc. Trained on massive text data to predict and generate language.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation, instead of relying purely on training data, the model looks up fresh info from a database before answering. Like open-book vs closed-book exam.
Embedding
A list of numbers (vector) that represents the meaning of text. Similar meanings = similar vectors. Powers semantic search and RAG retrieval.
Vector Database
A database optimized for storing and searching embeddings. Examples: Pinecone, Chroma, Weaviate. The storage layer under every RAG system.
Prompt Engineering
The craft of writing instructions that get an LLM to do exactly what you want. More important than it sounds, bad prompts get bad outputs.
Fine-tuning
Further training a pretrained model on your specific data to make it better at your specific task. More expensive than prompting but more precise.
Agent
An LLM that can take actions, call APIs, run code, search the web, use tools, not just generate text. The "doing" version of a chatbot.
Tool Calling
When an LLM decides to use an external function (a "tool") to get info or take action. The mechanism that makes agents actually useful.
Context Window
How much text an LLM can "see" at once. GPT-4 has ~128k tokens; Claude has ~200k. If your document is too long, it gets cut off.
Hallucination
When an LLM confidently makes up facts that aren't true. Not a bug, it's how they work. RAG and grounding help reduce it.
Temperature
A number (0 to 2) controlling how random the LLM's output is. Low = deterministic and focused. High = creative and unpredictable.
Token
The unit LLMs think in. Roughly 0.75 words. "hamburger" = 1 token, "antidisestablishmentarianism" = ~4 tokens. LLM costs are per-token.
Inference
Running a trained model to get predictions. "Inference time" = when the model is actually working for you. Opposite of training.
Semantic Search
Search that understands meaning rather than matching exact words. "puppy" matches "dog" results because they're semantically close.
Guardrails
Rules or filters that constrain what an AI agent can say or do. Prevents hallucinations, off-topic responses, or dangerous outputs.
Grounding
Connecting an LLM's responses to real, verifiable sources (documents, databases). Reduces hallucination and increases trustworthiness.
Human-in-the-Loop
AI workflows with a human checkpoint before critical decisions or actions. A safety pattern for high-stakes automation.
OCR
Optical Character Recognition, extracting text from images or scanned PDFs. Step 1 of almost every document processing pipeline.
NLP
Natural Language Processing, the broader field of making computers understand and generate human language. LLMs are the current state-of-the-art in NLP.
Multimodal
AI that can handle multiple types of input, text, images, audio, video. GPT-4V and Claude are multimodal models.
HyperAutomation
Gartner's term for combining multiple automation technologies (RPA, AI, APIs, BPM) to automate as many business processes as possible end-to-end.
RPA
Robotic Process Automation, software bots that mimic human actions in UIs (clicking, typing, copy-paste). Think: the older, non-AI sibling of AI agents.
Systems Integration
Connecting separate software systems (HR, Finance, CRM, etc.) so they share data and work together. Your actual job title's core meaning.
Stakeholder
Anyone with a stake in the project's outcome, business users, managers, executives. Managing their expectations is half the job.
Sprint
A fixed period (usually 2 weeks) in Agile development where the team commits to completing specific work. Stand-ups happen daily during sprints.
Scrum
An Agile framework with defined roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Dev Team), ceremonies (sprint planning, retrospective), and artifacts (backlog, burn-down chart).
Backlog
The prioritized list of all work items (features, bugs, tasks) for a project. "In the backlog" = acknowledged but not yet scheduled.
POC / Proof of Concept
A small, quick experiment to prove that an idea works before committing to a full build. You'll build a lot of these at Paramount.
MVP
Minimum Viable Product, the simplest version that delivers value. "What's the MVP?" = what's the smallest thing we can ship and still be useful?
KPI
Key Performance Indicator, a measurable metric that shows whether you're achieving goals. "What's the KPI?" = how will we know if this worked?
ROI
Return on Investment, did the benefit outweigh the cost? Every automation project at a company needs a clear ROI story to get approved.
SLA
Service Level Agreement, a contract defining expected response times, uptime, or quality. "What's the SLA?" = what are we promising and to whom?
Use Case
A specific scenario of how a system will be used. "The use case is..." = here's the exact problem we're solving, for this person, in this situation.
Change Management
The process of helping people adapt to new systems or workflows. Often the hardest part of automation, the tech is easy, humans are hard.
Governance
Rules, policies, and oversight structures for how systems or data are used. "AI Governance" = who decides what AI can/can't do in the company.
Sign-off
Formal approval from a stakeholder or manager that work is complete and acceptable. You need sign-off before most things go to production.
Bandwidth
Corporate-speak for available time and capacity. "I don't have the bandwidth" = I'm too busy. "Do you have bandwidth for this?" = are you free?
Leverage
Using existing resources, relationships, or tools to do more with less. "We can leverage our existing API" = we already have this, let's use it.
Sync
A meeting, usually short and informal. "Can we sync?" = can we meet/talk? "Let's sync on this" = let's align our understanding.
Action Item
A specific task assigned to a specific person with a due date that comes out of a meeting. "You're the action item owner on this."
API
Application Programming Interface, a defined way for software to talk to other software. "We'll call their API" = we'll send requests to their system and get data back.
REST API
The most common type of API. Uses HTTP verbs (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE) and returns JSON. Almost every modern service has a REST API.
URL vs URI
URL (Uniform Resource Locator) is the full web address with protocol (https://...). URI (Uniform Resource Identifier) is the broader concept, every URL is a URI, but not vice versa.
JSON
JavaScript Object Notation, the universal language for sending data between systems. Key-value pairs in { } curly braces. You'll read and write JSON constantly.
SDK
Software Development Kit, a pre-built library that makes it easier to use an API or platform. "Use the OpenAI SDK" = use their Python library instead of raw HTTP calls.
Webhook
A "reverse API", instead of you asking for data, the other system pushes data to you automatically when something happens. Events trigger webhooks.
Endpoint
A specific URL that an API exposes for a specific action. "The /users endpoint" = the URL you call to get user data from that API.
Container / Docker
A lightweight, portable package that includes your app and everything it needs to run, so it works the same on any machine. Docker is the standard tool.
Git / Version Control
A system that tracks every change to code over time. Git is the tool; GitHub/GitLab are hosting platforms. "Push to git" = save your code to the shared repo.
CI/CD
Continuous Integration / Continuous Deployment, automated pipelines that test and deploy code every time someone pushes a change. The assembly line for software.

💌 Tej's Tips

Personal notes from me to you. Read these when you're feeling lost or overwhelmed.

01
You don't need to understand everything before you start.
The biggest trap in learning technical stuff is waiting until you "really get it" before trying. You won't really get it until you try. Start things before you feel ready. The confusion is the learning, not a sign you're doing it wrong.
02
Ask questions loudly and early.
In corporate environments, people who ask good questions are seen as curious and engaged, not dumb. The person who sits quietly for three months pretending they understand everything is the one who struggles. Ask your manager, ask your team, ask in Slack. Every question you ask now saves you ten hours of confusion later.
03
Build something every week. Even something tiny.
Reading and watching is passive. Building is active. Even if it's a 20-line script that prints "hello world" after doing something mildly useful, build it. Your brain remembers things you made. Use the Playground section on this site. Run code in Colab. Break things and fix them. That's where the real learning happens.
04
Your non-technical background is a superpower, not a weakness.
Most engineers are terrible at explaining what they built to business people. You can do both sides. You understand what stakeholders actually want. You can translate between "the system needs a RAG pipeline with a 512-token chunk size" and "we need the chatbot to answer questions from our policy documents." That skill is genuinely rare. Use it.
05
Don't compare your progress to others. Compare to yourself from last week.
You'll meet people who've been coding for 10 years. You'll see GitHub profiles with hundreds of projects. That's noise. The only comparison that matters: are you better than you were last week? Keep a small log, even just a few sentences, of what you learned each week. Reading it back after a month is genuinely motivating.
06
This plan is a guide, not a prison.
If something on the plan is boring you, skip it or skim it. If something not on the plan is fascinating you, go deep. Curiosity is the engine. The plan exists to give you direction and confidence that you're covering the right things, not to be a rigid schedule. And if you fall behind? Just pick back up. No guilt. Starting over is always an option.
